The Law Society of Kenya lists over 18,000 practicing advocates, yet individuals and small businesses often struggle to find legal professionals matched to their specific needs. BackgroundAccess to legal representation in Kenya and across much of Sub-Saharan Africa has historically been shaped by personal connections, social networks, and referrals rather than objective assessments of a lawyer's competence or suitability for a given case. The Law Society of Kenya (LSK) currently lists over 18,000 practicing advocates (Law Society of Kenya, 2024), yet many individuals and small businesses struggle to identify legal professionals suited to their specific needs. Most clients rely on word-of-mouth recommendations, which disproportionately advantages well-connected lawyers regardless of their actual expertise or track record.Globally, technology has increasingly been leveraged to address information asymmetries in professional service markets. Online platforms such as Upwork for freelancers, Zocdoc for medical professionals, and LegalMatch in the United States have demonstrated that structured digital marketplaces can effectively match service seekers with qualified providers (LegalMatch, 2026). These platforms use profile-based matching, ratings, and reviews to promote transparency and enable informed decision-making.In Kenya, the legal services sector remains largely fragmented and opaque. The absence of a structured, technology-enabled platform means that clients cannot easily evaluate lawyers based on verifiable credentials, specialization, or performance history. This creates a market where social capital, rather than competence, drives client acquisition for lawyers. The rapid growth of internet access in Kenya, with internet penetration exceeding 40% of the population and a growing base of desktop and laptop users (Communications Authority of Kenya, 2023), presents a significant opportunity to deploy a web-based solution that can bridge this gap.A web platform that enables clients to describe their legal issues and be algorithmically matched to lawyers based on specialization and profile descriptions has the potential to fundamentally democratize access to legal services. By incorporating transparent ratings and peer reviews, such a platform can ensure that merit, rather than social connections, drives legal service discovery in Kenya.1.2 Problem StatementThe current legal services landscape in Kenya lacks a structured, transparent, and accessible mechanism through which clients can discover and evaluate lawyers based on verified qualifications, areas of specialization, and performance records. Clients are therefore unable to make informed decisions when seeking legal representation, often resulting in mismatches between a lawyer's ability and the client's actual legal needs.Furthermore, junior and independently practicing lawyers without established professional networks are systematically disadvantaged in attracting clients, despite potentially possessing the requisite skills and knowledge. This opacity perpetuates inequality within the legal profession and undermines public trust in the justice system. Without a merit-based discovery mechanism, both clients and competent lawyers suffer from poor matching outcomes, wasted resources, and diminished access to justice.1.3 ObjectivesThis section profiles correspond to those issues.To design and develop a web application incorporating a text-matching algorithm, lawyer profile creation, and a peer-review rating system.To test and evaluate the platform's matching accuracy, usability, and effectiveness in improving client-lawyer connections.1.3.3 Research QuestionsWhat are the key shortcomings of the current legal service discovery landscape in Kenya in facilitating effective client-lawyer matching?What challenges do clients and lawyers face in the current landscape, and how have existing technological solutions addressed representation is important for multiple stakeholders. For clients, a merit-based platform reduces the risk of poor matches, lowers the time and cost of finding appropriate legal help, and improves outcomes for their cases. For junior and independent lawyers, particularly those without established networks, the platform provides equitable visibility proportional to their actual skills and client satisfaction records.For the legal profession broadly, a transparent marketplace promotes accountability and encourages lawyers to maintain high standards of service. Indirect beneficiaries include the Kenyan justice system, which benefits when citizens can more readily access competent legal representation, and regulatory bodies such as the LSK, which have a mandate to uphold professional standards. The proposed platform directly addresses these needs by introducing a structured, technology-driven, and client-centered mechanism for legal service discovery.1.5 ScopeThe proposed project focuses on designing and developing a web-based platform for client-lawyer matching in Kenya. The platform will include a lawyer registration and profile management module, a client-side module for describing legal issues and receiving match recommendations, a text-matching engine that compares client descriptions against lawyer profiles, and a rating and review system. The primary stakeholders are individual clients seeking legal services and practicing lawyers registered with the LSK. The platform will initially support the most common legal specializations in Kenya, including family law, property law, criminal law, employment law, and commercial law.1.6 LimitationsIdeally, a Bayesian Network approach would have been employed to model probabilistic relationships between case characteristics, legal specializations, and historical outcomes to produce more nuanced and accurate lawyer-client matches. However, this approach was not feasible for three key reasons: the development team does not currently possess the statistical and machine learning expertise required to implement such a model; the training of a Bayesian Network requires access to large volumes of historical legal case data, which is not publicly available in Kenya; and the project timeline does not accommodate the extended development and training cycles that such a model would require.Additionally, the platform's effectiveness depends on lawyers actively maintaining accurate and detailed profiles. Low lawyer adoption during the initial launch phase may limit the breadth of matching options available to clients.1.7 DelimitationsGiven the limitations described above, the project will implement a text-based matching approach in which clients describe their legal issues in natural language, and the system computes similarity scores between these descriptions and the textual content of lawyer profiles, including stated specializations, areas of practice, and descriptions of previously handled case types. This approach leverages established natural language processing (NLP) techniques such as TF-IDF and cosine similarity, which are well within the team's current competence and can be implemented within the project's timeframe.The platform will initially target the Kenyan market only and will not extend to other Sub-Saharan African jurisdictions in this phase. Verification of lawyer credentials will rely on LSK registration numbers provided by the lawyers themselves, with no automated integration with LSK systems in this iteration.Chapter 2: Literature Review2.1 IntroductionThis chapter examines the landscape of legal service discovery, reviews the current mechanisms through which clients in Kenya find legal representation, identifies the challenges inherent in those mechanisms, surveys related applications that have attempted to address similar problems, and identifies the gaps that the proposed platform seeks to fill. The chapter also reviews key technologies relevant to the proposed system.2.2 Legal Service Discovery in KenyaIn Kenya, legal representation is an essential component of access to justice, yet the mechanisms through which clients identify and retain lawyers remain largely informal. A significant proportion of individuals and businesses find legal representation through personal referrals, relying on the networks of family members, colleagues, or business associates to recommend advocates (Law Society of Kenya, 2024). This approach inherently privileges clients who are socially well-connected and disadvantages those from lower socioeconomic backgrounds or rural areas who may not have access to such networks.Globally, the shift towards digital platforms for professional service discovery has been significant. A study by the World Justice Project (2022) found that access to legal services is one of the most persistent justice gaps, with millions of people worldwide unable to access affordable and qualified legal help. Technology-enabled platforms have begun to address this gap in more developed markets, but Sub-Saharan Africa, including Kenya, remains underserved by such solutions.The LSK maintains a publicly accessible directory of practicing advocates, but this directory functions merely as a contact list and provides no mechanism for clients to assess a lawyer's suitability for their specific legal needs (Law Society of Kenya, 2024). It offers no specialization filtering, performance metrics, or client reviews, making it an insufficient tool for informed decision-making. The result is a fragmented marketplace where lawyer selection is driven by social capital rather than merit.2.3 Current Client-Lawyer Matching Approaches in KenyaThe dominant approach to client-lawyer matching in Kenya involves informal social referrals supplemented by physical walk-ins to law firm offices. Some law firms maintain websites that list their areas of practice, but these are primarily marketing tools rather than interactive matching systems. There is no established digital platform in Kenya that allows clients to describe their legal needs and be algorithmically matched to suitable lawyers.The limitations of these approaches are well-documented. Informal referrals can result in mismatches between a lawyer's actual expertise and a client's needs, particularly in specialized areas of law. They also perpetuate systemic inequities by advantaging lawyers with stronger social networks regardless of competence (Genn, 2019). The absence of performance-based visibility mechanisms means that junior lawyers with strong skills but limited networks cannot compete effectively for clients.2.4 Challenges in the Current ApproachesThe current landscape presents several distinct challenges. First, information asymmetry is severe: clients lack access to verifiable data about a lawyer's specialization, track record, or client satisfaction, making it difficult to assess suitability before engagement (World Justice Project, 2022). Second, social capital bias systematically advantages well-connected lawyers over potentially more competent ones with smaller networks. Third, the absence of a feedback mechanism means that poor-performing lawyers face no reputational consequences in the current informal market, reducing accountability. Fourth, junior lawyers entering the profession face significant structural barriers to attracting clients, limiting the diversity and dynamism of the legal marketplace.2.5 Related ApplicationsSeveral technology platforms in other jurisdictions have attempted to address the challenge of connecting clients with legal professionals in a structured, transparent, and technology-mediated manner. This section reviews three such applications, paying particular attention to their use of text-based matching and profile-driven discovery.2.5.1 LegalMatchLegalMatch is a United States-based online legal matching service that connects individuals and businesses with licensed attorneys (LegalMatch, 2026). Clients submit a description of their legal issue through an online intake form, and the platform's system uses this description to match them with member attorneys based on location, practice area, and other criteria. Attorneys review anonymized summaries of client cases and decide whether to respond. The service is free for consumers, with attorneys paying a subscription fee to access leads. LegalMatch has facilitated matches for more than seven million legal cases and represents one of the most established examples of text-description-based client-lawyer matching (LegalMatch, 2026).The platform's core matching mechanism is directly relevant to the proposed system: clients articulate their needs in natural language, and the system uses that articulation to identify relevant lawyers. However, LegalMatch is limited to the United States and operates primarily as a lead generation service rather than a fully transparent merit-based marketplace with public ratings visible to all users.2.5.2 AvvoAvvo is a legal directory and marketplace launched in 2006 that hosts profiles for approximately 97% of all licensed attorneys in the United States (Avvo, 2026). Avvo assigns each attorney a rating between 1 and 10 based on a proprietary algorithm that evaluates profile information, awards, publications, and peer endorsements. Clients can search for lawyers by location and practice area, read client reviews, and contact lawyers directly through the platform. The platform blends directory functionality with a structured rating system and a legal Q&A forum.Avvo's relevance to the proposed system lies in its demonstration that a community-driven rating and review mechanism can meaningfully supplement profile-based matching, enabling clients to make more informed decisions about legal representation. However, Avvo's matching is primarily search-and-filter-based rather than algorithmically driven by a description of the client's legal issue, which limits its ability to surface nuanced matches.2.5.3 Using Bayesian Networks and Machine Learning to Predict Computer Science SuccessNudelman, Moodley, and Berman (2018) from the University of Cape Town developed a system that applies Bayesian Networks to predict academic outcomes in Computer Science programmes. The system models probabilistic relationships between student background variables and academic performance indicators to produce outcome predictions. While this application is in education rather than legal services, it demonstrates the power of probabilistic modelling in matching and prediction tasks where multiple variables must be weighed simultaneously.This work is relevant as it represents the kind of matching sophistication that the proposed platform aspired to implement. As described in Section 1.6, the use of Bayesian Networks for lawyer-client matching was considered but ultimately deemed infeasible within the current project's constraints of time, expertise, and data availability. The system by Nudelman et al. (2018) illustrates both the potential and the demands of such an approach.2.6 Gaps in Current Approaches and Related ApplicationsThe review of current approaches and related applications reveals several important gaps. LegalMatch, while pioneering text-description-based matching, is geographically restricted to the United States and is not designed for the Kenyan legal context, where accessible web-based solutions are critical. Avvo operates primarily as a directory with search-and-filter functionality rather than an NLP-driven matching system that processes natural language descriptions of client needs. Neither platform serves the Kenyan market, where informal referrals dominate and no technology-enabled alternative exists.The Bayesian Network approach reviewed through the work of Nudelman et al. (2018) offers a theoretically superior matching mechanism but requires historical case data and significant machine learning expertise that are not available in the current project’s context. There is therefore a clear gap for a web-based, NLP-driven, merit-based legal matching platform tailored to the Kenyan market.2.7 Technologies Related to the Study2.7.1 Natural Language Processing and Text MatchingNatural language processing (NLP) encompasses a range of computational techniques for analyzing, understanding, and generating human language. For the proposed system, the most relevant NLP techniques are those used for text similarity and matching. TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) is a widely used weighting scheme that reflects how important a word is to a document within a corpus (Ramos, 2003). When combined with cosine similarity, TF-IDF can be used to compute a numerical similarity score between a client's issue description and a lawyer's profile text, enabling the system to rank lawyers by relevance to a given client query.More advanced approaches include word embeddings such as Word2Vec and GloVe, which represent words as dense vectors in a high-dimensional space, capturing semantic relationships between terms. These can enhance matching quality by identifying semantically related terms even when exact keyword overlap is limited (Mikolov et al., 2013). For the initial implementation, TF-IDF with cosine similarity provides a robust and computationally efficient baseline that is well within the project's technical scope.2.7.2 React NativeReact.js is an open-source JavaScript library developed by Meta for building dynamic, component-based user interfaces for web applications (Meta, 2023). React.js enables efficient rendering and state management across complex single-page applications, reducing development time and improving maintainability. Its component-based architecture supports the creation of interactive user interfaces such as profile cards, search results, and review forms. Given that the proposed platform targets users accessing the service via web browsers on desktop and laptop devices, React.js’s robust ecosystem, wide browser compatibility, and large developer community make it an appropriate choice for front-end development.2.7.3 PostgreSQL with pgvectorPostgreSQL is a robust, open-source relational database management system that provides the strict ACID compliance and schema enforcement necessary for managing complex, interconnected data like users, cases, and reviews. Unlike traditional NoSQL solutions, PostgreSQL supports the pgvector extension, enabling the database to natively store and index high-dimensional vectors. This allows the core matching engine to execute complex SQL metadata filtering and mathematical cosine similarity calculations simultaneously within a single, highly optimized query. By combining relational integrity with advanced vector capabilities, PostgreSQL eliminates data fragmentation and ensures predictable performance as the platform scales.2.8 Conceptual FrameworkThe proposed system operates through three primary actors: clients, lawyers, and administrators. Lawyers register on the platform and create detailed profiles that include their LSK registration number, academic qualifications, years of experience, areas of legal specialization, and descriptions of case types they have handled. These profile descriptions are stored in the database and indexed for text matching.Clients register and describe their legal issues in a free-text input field. The text-matching engine computes similarity scores between the client's description and each lawyer's profile text using TF-IDF and cosine similarity, returning a ranked list of recommended lawyers. Clients can browse recommended lawyer profiles, read ratings and reviews left by previous clients, and initiate contact with a selected lawyer through the platform.After an engagement, clients can submit a rating and review for the lawyer, which updates the lawyer's visible performance metrics. Administrators can monitor the platform, verify LSK registration numbers, and moderate content. Two categories of requirements are identified for the LegalMatchingPlatform: functional requirements, which define what the system must do, and non-functional requirements, which constrain how the system must perform.4.2.1 Functional RequirementsFunctional requirements describe the specific behaviours and functions the system must support in order to meet user needs. The following table presents the functional requirements of the LegalMatchingPlatform, categorised by the type of function they fulfil.RequirementDescriptionFR-01: User Registration & AuthenticationThe system shall allow clients and lawyers to register using their email address and a secure password. Lawyers shall additionally provide their Law Society of Kenya (LSK) registration number during signup. The system shall authenticate users upon login and maintain role-based sessions distinguishing clients, lawyers, and administrators.FR-02: Lawyer Profile ManagementThe system shall allow registered lawyers to create and update a detailed profile that includes their LSK number, full name, biography, areas of specialisation, years of experience, educational background, and case history. The system shall automatically construct a searchable text blob (profileText) from these fields to support TF-IDF indexing.FR-03: Legal Issue SubmissionThe system shall allow clients to submit a legal issue by providing a free-text description, legal category, jurisdiction, urgency level, and budget range. The submitted issue shall be stored as a ClientIssue record and assigned a status of pending upon creation.FR-04: Text-Based Lawyer MatchingThe system shall employ a TF-IDF algorithm to compute cosine similarity scores between a client's submitted issue and all indexed lawyer profiles. The system shall rank matched lawyers in descending order of similarity score and present the ranked list to the client. The matching process shall be triggered automatically upon issue submission.FR-05: Match Results & Lawyer SelectionThe system shall display a ranked list of matched lawyers to the client, including each lawyer's name, specialisation, similarity score, and summary statistics. The client shall be able to view a detailed profile for any matched lawyer and confirm a selection. Upon selection, the lawyer shall be notified and the client's case details shall be shared with the selected lawyer.FR-06: Review & Rating SubmissionThe system shall allow a client to submit a review for a lawyer following the conclusion of an engagement. Each review shall include a numerical rating (1 to 5) and an optional free-text comment. The system shall update the lawyer's average rating and total review count upon each new review submission.FR-07: Administrator FunctionsThe system shall provide an administrator dashboard through which authorised administrators can: verify lawyer credentials and toggle profile visibility; moderate flagged reviews by approving or removing them; deactivate user accounts; and generate platform analytics reports covering user counts, case statistics, average match scores, and review distributions.FR-08: Analytics & ReportingThe system shall generate analytics reports on demand, capturing metrics including total users, total lawyers, total clients, total issues submitted, total matches generated, total reviews, average cosine similarity score, average lawyer rating, and breakdowns by legal category and case status.FR-09: NotificationsThe system shall notify lawyers when a client selects them from a match list. The system shall notify clients when a lawyer responds to a match request. Notifications shall be delivered through the in-platform messaging interface.FR-10: Data Security & Access ControlThe system shall enforce role-based access control so that clients may only access their own cases and matches, lawyers may only access their own profile and matched cases, and administrators may access all records subject to their assigned permissions. No unauthenticated user shall access protected resources.4.2.2 Non-Functional RequirementsNon-functional requirements specify the operational qualities and constraints of the system. They govern how the system performs its functions rather than what those functions are.Performance and Scalability. The system shall return match results within three seconds of issue submission under normal load. The TF-IDF engine shall be capable of indexing a minimum of 10,000 lawyer profiles without degradation in query response time. PostgreSQL, optimized with pgvector indexing algorithms (such as HNSW), shall be leveraged to execute rapid vector similarity searches alongside traditional SQL metadata queries, accommodating continuous growth in concurrent users and data volume.Reliability and Availability. The system shall maintain an uptime of at least 99.5% per month, utilizing a reliable cloud-hosted PostgreSQL database instance with automated daily backups and high-availability configurations. In the event of a service interruption, the system shall display an appropriate error message and preserve all user data through strict ACID transactional guarantees. No data shall be lost due to a system failure.Security. All user data shall be transmitted over HTTPS. Passwords shall be cryptographically hashed using a strong, industry-standard algorithm (such as bcrypt) before storage in the PostgreSQL database and shall never be stored in plaintext. Access control policies and backend middleware shall enforce role-based data access at the database level, ensuring that database queries strictly prevent unauthorized reads or writes. Case details shall only be disclosed to a lawyer upon explicit client selection.Usability. The system interface shall adhere to responsive design principles, ensuring full functionality on both desktop and mobile devices. All primary user workflows, including issue submission and match review, shall be completable within four navigational steps. Error messages shall be descriptive and guide the user towards corrective action.Portability and Compatibility. The platform shall function correctly on the latest versions of Google Chrome, Mozilla Firefox, Microsoft Edge, and Safari. The system shall not require any client-side software installation beyond a standard web browser.Maintainability. The codebase shall follow component-based architecture principles, separating concerns across the authentication, matching, profile, and administration modules. All public functions and components shall be documented inline. The system shall be deployable via a standard CI/CD pipeline without manual server configuration.Legal and Regulatory Compliance. The system shall comply with the Kenya Data Protection Act (2019) with respect to the collection, storage, and processing of personal data. Lawyer verification shall align with the requirements of the Law Society of Kenya Act (Cap. 18), ensuring only duly registered advocates appear in match results.4.3 System AnalysisSystem analysis is the process of studying a system to determine the optimal way it can be modelled to produce a solution that best meets the identified requirements.
